Overview

The Industrial Line Redesign Project was completed as part of a professional Lean Systems Engineering course and focused on improving material and information flow within an active manufacturing operation producing fireplace mantles. The engagement began with an on-site visit to a manufacturer that had recently launched a new production line and was experiencing inefficiencies driven by batching, waiting, and limited flow control.

Our team was tasked with documenting the current-state production system and designing a target-state system that improved efficiency and reduced total lead time. Required deliverables included a current-state MIFC, a redesigned target-state MIFC, and a formal A3 summarizing the analysis, countermeasures, and projected outcomes.

I served as the team lead for eight students, coordinated fieldwork and analysis, and completed the majority of the technical deliverables. That included developing the target-state MIFC, aligning countermeasures into a cohesive design, and ensuring every recommendation reflected realistic constraints.

The project concluded with a formal presentation delivered to the host company and an audience of approximately 50 industry professionals and faculty members, showcasing the lean analysis artifacts and implementation roadmap.

Goals

  • Design a target-state system with improved flow and efficiency

  • Ensure proposed changes were realistic and implementable
  • Clearly communicate findings to academic and industry audiences

System / Architecture

The production system spanned sequential cutting, assembly, finishing, drying, packing, and shipping operations. Material moved in large batches with minimal visual management, so finished work piled up between stations while upstream teams rushed to meet forecasts, amplifying queues and starving downstream processes.

The target-state architecture replaced uncontrolled batching with defined FIFO lanes, smaller lot sizes, and explicit WIP limits between major processes. Information flow was clarified through simple visual controls and standardized handoffs, enabling operators to pull work when capacity was available instead of pushing batches forward blindly.

All proposed changes were scoped to fit within existing equipment, layout, and staffing constraints. The redesign emphasized practical sequencing rules, clear ownership of each buffer, and visual indicators that could be implemented quickly without large capital investments.

Execution & Build Log

Execution

Phase 1 — Current-State Analysis

  • Observed production on site and gathered cycle-time samples for every process.
  • Documented material and information flow to expose bottlenecks and ownership gaps.
  • Built the current-state MIFC highlighting queue drivers and system triggers.

Phase 2 — Target-State Design

  • Identified major sources of waste and imbalance across the line.
  • Developed a target-state MIFC with smaller lot sizes, FIFO lanes, and WIP limits.
  • Prioritized countermeasures that respected equipment, staffing, and layout reality.

Phase 3 — A3 Development

  • Consolidated the analysis, countermeasures, and projected outcomes into a formal A3.
  • Linked lead-time reductions and queue improvements directly to proposed changes.
  • Captured implementation considerations, owners, and next steps.

Phase 4 — Presentation & Review

  • Presented findings to the host company plus an audience of industry professionals.
  • Facilitated Q&A with operations, engineering, and leadership stakeholders.
  • Delivered a concise action plan outlining sequencing and follow-up recommendations.

Results & Outcomes

  • Reduced total system lead time by approximately 66%.
  • Cut queue-driven delays by roughly 72%.
  • Eliminated two of the three major bottlenecks across the line.
  • Identified drying time as the next primary improvement opportunity.
