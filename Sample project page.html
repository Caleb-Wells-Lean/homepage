Overview

During my internship at Overland Conveying, I was assigned to the redesign and replacement of an existing coal chute conveyor system inside an active coal-fired power plant. The chute, measuring approximately 80 feet in length, transfers raw coal between primary conveyors and downstream processing equipment and is a critical link in the plantâ€™s fuel handling system.

The original chute had been in service for decades and showed significant wear, structural fatigue, and flow issues caused by outdated design standards and limited maintenance access. Compounding the issue, the available documentation consisted of incomplete CAD files, deteriorated paper drawings, and handwritten revisions that were never formally incorporated into a controlled drawing set.

My responsibility was to fully reverse-engineer the existing system, verify its geometry using field measurements and archived records, and produce a modern, fabrication-ready drawing package. The goal was not only to replace the worn chute, but to improve durability, simplify maintenance, and ensure the new design could be fabricated and installed with minimal disruption to plant operations.

This project required careful coordination between design, fabrication, and field installation teams, and served as a practical example of modernizing legacy industrial infrastructure through disciplined documentation and engineering standards.

System / Architecture

The coal chute system was rebuilt digitally as a modular steel assembly designed to integrate with existing conveyor infrastructure. The overall chute was divided into defined subassemblies to simplify fabrication, transport, and on-site installation while maintaining structural continuity.

Large welded plate sections were broken into manageable modules connected through standardized bolt patterns and reinforced flanges. These interfaces allowed sections to be aligned, assembled, and serviced more easily than the original monolithic design. Access points and removable panels were added at key locations to support inspection and maintenance without full disassembly.

Internal geometry was refined to improve material flow and reduce wear in high-impact areas. Transition zones, corners, and discharge regions were adjusted to minimize coal buildup and abrasion while preserving alignment with upstream and downstream conveyors. All changes were made within the constraints of the existing support structure and mounting locations to avoid unnecessary plant modifications.

Execution & Build Log

Execution

Phase 1 — Reverse Engineering

  • Reviewed paper drawings, scanned files, and handwritten revisions
  • Verified dimensions using records and field measurements
  • Recreated the full assembly in AutoCAD as a baseline

Phase 2 — Design Updates

  • Standardized connections and bolt patterns
  • Added wear plates, smoother transitions, and access points
  • Coordinated design decisions with senior engineers

Phase 3 — Fabrication Support

  • Produced complete 2D fabrication drawings
  • Reviewed drawings with the shop and clarified fit-up details
  • Issued quick revisions as fabrication constraints appeared

Phase 4 — Installation Support

  • Assisted with assembly planning and on-site fit-up
  • Verified alignment and integration during installation
